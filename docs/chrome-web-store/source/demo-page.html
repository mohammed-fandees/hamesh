The Architecture of Memory

12 min read

Encoding is the process by which perceived information is converted into a construct that can be stored, later reconstructed, and retrieved from long-term memory. It is the first of three stages memory researchers describe — encoding, storage, and retrieval — and arguably the one most within a learner's conscious control.

The retrieval-practice effect, sometimes called the testing effect, shows that actively recalling information strengthens memory far more effectively than passively re-reading the same material. Students who quiz themselves outperform students who simply reread their notes, even when the rereading group spends more total time studying.

Why context matters

A thought rarely arrives in isolation. It is usually a reaction to something specific — a sentence, an argument, a number that doesn't add up. Stripped of that anchor, the thought drifts. Reconnecting a note to the exact passage that produced it turns out to matter more than the note's content alone.
